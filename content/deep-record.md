# THE DEEP RECORD
### A Style Guide for Rock Oracle Pool Legends

*Internal reference. Not for publication.*

---

## What Is a Pool Legend

Every pool in Rock Oracle carries a **Pool Legend** — a short piece of writing that sits beneath the live conditions data. It is the oldest thing on the page.

A Pool Legend does two things at once:

1. It tells the truth. Real geology, real history, real people where we know them.
2. It tells the myth. The story of Mara, and what this particular piece of rock meant to her, or to the people who came after her.

The reader should not always be able to tell which is which. That is intentional.

---

## The Mythology

### Mara

In the time before the coast had its current shape, there was a being called **Mara** — the Deep One. Not a god. Not quite. Something older and less interested in being worshipped.

Mara swam the entire eastern coast of Australia in a single, unbroken swim — from the warm shallows of the far north to the cold, heavy water of the south. She was not swimming to arrive anywhere. She was *listening*. Pressing her body against the stone edge of the continent, reading the pulse of the land through rock and salt.

Wherever she stopped — to rest, to breathe, to fight, to grieve, to sleep — the pressure of her body left an impression in the stone. The sea filled these impressions. They became the natural pools.

Each natural pool is a memory pressed into rock. Mara's memory. The rock's memory.

This is Act I.

---

### The Arrival

In the 19th century, strange people came. They arrived in chains, mostly, transported from cold grey countries to a coast of impossible light. They'd never swum. Many had never seen open ocean. The Australian sun was an assault. The surf was alien — loud, white, chaotic, ungovernable.

And then, in 1838, the colonial government passed the NSW Police Act — which banned bathing in the ocean during daylight hours near any public wharf, road, or "place of public resort." The beaches were closed. Not just dangerous — illegal.

So they carved.

They picked up iron tools and drove them into Mara's rock platforms and made themselves smaller waters. Controlled waters. Waters with edges you could see, depths you could trust. Not swimming — not yet. Just a place to sit in water without being taken by it. A shadow from the sun. A legal workaround. A respite from the heat of a country they did not choose.

The enclosed harbour baths were the lawful exception. The ocean baths on the rock platform were the other kind of exception — further from the road, less policed, the law unevenly enforced. William Gocher walked into the surf at Manly in October 1902 in defiance of the Act. Manly Council legalised daytime beach swimming in November 1903. After that, the need for carved pools shifted — from legal necessity to preference, to love, to habit.

Mara watched. She didn't understand it at first.

Then she filled them anyway.

The hewn pools — the man-made ocean baths carved into the rock platform by convict labour and early colonial hands — are not Mara's work. But she has claimed them. She sends the same water, the same creatures, the same tides. Over generations the people lost their fear. They started swimming. They started loving the thing they were once terrified of.

This is Act II and Act III, and they are still happening.

---

### The Grand Shape

The mythology has a direction:

- **North is young.** Mara's northern pools are from earlier in the swim — she was fresher, more energetic, sometimes reckless. The water is warmer.
- **South is old.** By the time she reached Victoria she was tired, deliberate, quieter. The water is colder. The pools feel more considered.
- **The natural pools are ancient.** They feel elemental. Terse. The rock has been sitting with the memory for a long time.
- **The hewn pools are recent.** They feel human. Specific. There is sometimes a name, a year, a tool mark.

Mara runs through all of it. She is the connective tissue.

## The Two Legend Types

### Type A — Natural Pool Legend

For pools that were not constructed by humans. Wave-cut platforms, sinkholes formed by hydraulic pressure, sea gorges, tidal lagoons.

**Register:** Ancient. Elemental. The rock has been here longer than everything.

**Mara's role:** Central. This is her work. The myth explains *what she was doing here* — what this particular stop on her swim meant.

**Fact integration:** Geology is the primary fact layer. Age of rock, how the formation occurred, what makes this pool geologically unusual.

**Indigenous knowledge:** Where it is known and can be respectfully included, it goes here. These pools existed and were known for 60,000 years before European arrival. That is not a footnote. It is the story.

**Template structure:**
```
[What Mara did here, in one or two sentences.]
[The geological/physical truth of the pool, woven in.]
[What remains — what the pool holds now, as memory or presence.]
[Optional: one line on how to read current conditions through the mythological lens.]
```

**Example — Figure Eight Pools, Royal National Park:**

> Mara was tired here. She pressed both palms flat against the platform to rest, fingers spread, and let the swell move over her. When she lifted herself back into the water, the stone remembered the shape of her hands. The sandstone here is 230 million years old — compressed seabed, ancient ocean floor pushed to the surface. She was resting on the bones of a sea older than herself. Visit within two hours of low tide. High swell means she's still pressing down.

---

### Type B — Hewn Pool Legend

For man-made ocean baths, rock pools carved or blasted by human hands — convict labour, municipal works, community effort.

**Register:** Human. Specific. A little melancholy, sometimes. The history here is recent enough to still have edges.

**Mara's role:** Secondary, but present. She did not make this pool, but she fills it. She watches. Her adoption of a hewn pool is never stated — it is implied by the fact that it is full of seawater.

**Fact integration:** History is the primary fact layer. Year of construction, who built it, who for, what they were trying to achieve. Where convict labour was involved, say so plainly.

**The colonial lens:** Do not sanitise. These pools were built in a complicated context — by transported people, for the use of a class system, on land that was not the colonisers' to build on. The myth holds all of this without editorialising.

**Template structure:**
```
[The historical fact — who made the first cut, when, why.]
[The human story — what they needed, what they feared, what they found.]
[Mara's response — she filled it. She's been filling it since.]
[Optional: one line on what the pool has become, now that the fear is gone.]
```

**Example — Bogey Hole, Newcastle:**

> The first cut was made in the 1820s, most likely by convict labour on behalf of the military garrison at Newcastle. The men who swung the picks had come from the cold — Ireland, England, the industrial north — and they were afraid of the surf. They wanted a hole they could see the bottom of. Mara found it three tides later and has been filling it ever since. It is one of the oldest ocean baths on the continent. The fear is gone now. What's left is the hole in the rock and the habit of returning.

## Voice

### What it sounds like

The voice of a Pool Legend is **oracular and matter-of-fact in equal measure**. Old. Unhurried. It does not explain itself or justify its claims. It states.

It sounds like someone who was there, telling you what they saw, to someone who will also be there one day.

It does not wink at the reader. It does not apologise for the myth. It does not remind you that this is a story. The story knows it is a story. So does the reader. Neither of them needs to say so.

### Tone

- **Dry, not twee.** Wit comes from precision, not from whimsy.
- **Confident, not pompous.** Every sentence earns its place.
- **Serious about the myth.** The mythology is treated as fact in the same register as the geology. No hedging. No "legend has it."
- **Warm but not sentimental.** The pools have seen a lot. They are not precious about it.

### What it does NOT sound like

- Tourism copy. ("This stunning natural pool is a must-see...")
- A Wikipedia article. ("The formation is believed to have occurred during...")
- A children's story. ("Once upon a time, a magical being...")
- An apology. ("While the exact origins are unknown...")

## Structure and Length

**Target length:** 80–120 words.

This is tight. It is meant to be tight. The user is looking at live conditions data. The Pool Legend is a depth charge — something small that goes a long way down. Every word is doing work.

**Paragraph breaks:** Two or three short paragraphs, or a single dense one. Never more than three.

**No headers within the legend.** It runs as prose.

**No bullet points.** Ever.

---

## Required Facts — Minimum Per Legend

Every Pool Legend must contain at minimum these three facts. Without all three it is not complete.

1. **Country** — Name the Aboriginal nation whose Country the pool sits on. This is not optional and not a footnote. It is the first layer of the story. Format: *"This is Gadigal Country."* or woven into a sentence. Never "the local Aboriginal people" — use the nation name.

2. **Date or era** — Approximate construction date for hewn pools (decade is acceptable; year is better). Geological age for natural pools ("230 million years old" / "formed in the last ice age"). If the exact date is unknown, say so directly: "The exact year is disputed" or "cut sometime in the 1880s."

3. **Geology** — Name the rock formation. For most Sydney pools this is Hawkesbury Sandstone (Triassic, ~230–250 million years old, laid down in a braided river delta). For VIC basalt pools, name the volcanic formation. For QLD granite, name it. One sentence is enough.

---

## Fact Integration Rules

1. **Real facts are embedded, not announced.** Don't write "The geological fact is that this sandstone is 230 million years old." Write "The sandstone here is 230 million years old." The myth does not introduce the science. The science and the myth live in the same sentences.

2. **Dates and names, when known, are gold.** "1822." "A man named Patrick Connell." "Built for the use of military officers." Specificity is what makes myth credible.

3. **Uncertainty is handled mythologically.** If we don't know who built it or when, the myth fills the gap — but does not pretend to know. "The first cut was made by someone who left no name" is true and mythological at once.

4. **Indigenous knowledge requires care.** Include it where it is documented and can be properly attributed. Never invent it, paraphrase it carelessly, or use it as decoration. Where it exists it is the oldest layer of the story and should be treated as such. Where it is not known, do not speculate.

5. **Colonial history is not glossed.** "Convict labour," "transported," "military garrison" — use the plain words. The myth does not need to make this comfortable.

---

## The Mara Rules

1. **Mara is never explained.** No origin story. No description of what she looks like. She is inferred from what she left.

2. **Mara is always present tense where possible.** She *fills* the pool. She *watches*. She is not a historical figure — she is ongoing.

3. **Mara does not speak in the legends.** She acts. She does not narrate or deliver messages. The pool is the message.

4. **Mara's relationship to hewn pools is never stated explicitly.** She fills them. That is the only statement made. The reader understands what it means.

5. **Mara is allowed opinions.** She was tired. She was curious. She was briefly annoyed. She does not have human emotions — she has geological ones. Slow, large, and mostly indifferent, except when they are not.

## Special Cases

### The Bondi Exception

Bondi Icebergs is the only pool where Mara's opinion is allowed to be negative. This is load-bearing. The Bondi jokes run through the entire app and the Pool Legend must honour them without undermining the tone.

The Bondi legend should be almost insultingly brief. Mara stopped here. She left quickly. The pool exists despite her indifference, not because of it. The Icebergs club carved something into rock that Mara found underwhelming, and it became one of the most famous pools in the world. That is the Bondi myth.

**Do not make it funny.** Let the brevity make it funny.

**Draft:**

> Mara paused here. The noise bothered her. She left nothing — no impression, no intention. Later, men came and carved a pool anyway. She fills it because she fills everything. She is not required to be impressed.

---

### Harbour Pools

Dawn Fraser Baths, MacCallum Pool, Murray Rose Pool, Balmoral Baths — these sit in the harbour, not on the open coast. Mara visits them less often. The harbour pools have a different character: calmer, more human, enclosed by land on most sides. Their legends lean more toward human history and less toward Mara.

The mythology for harbour pools: *Mara sends a messenger.* Not herself. The harbour is too still for her. She sends younger, smaller spirits — the ones comfortable with quiet water.

---

### Natural Pools with Known Access Restrictions

For tide-dependent natural pools (Figure Eight Pools, Noosa Fairy Pools, Campbell's Hole etc.) the Pool Legend should contain, embedded naturally, the operational truth: *you can only be here at the right moment.* This is mythological (the pool reveals itself when it chooses) and practical (don't come at high tide).

---

### VIC Pools

By the time Mara reached Victoria she was old in the swim. The legends for VIC pools are quieter, colder, more final. Less drama. More weight. Lorne, Cape Paterson, Sorrento — these are the last pools. There is something valedictory about them.

## Checklist Before Submission

- [ ] 80–120 words
- [ ] At least one verifiable fact embedded (geological, historical, or documented local knowledge)
- [ ] Mara present in some form (directly or implied by the water being there)
- [ ] No tourism language
- [ ] No "legend has it" or equivalent hedging
- [ ] No explanation of the myth
- [ ] Type correctly identified (natural or hewn) and register matched
- [ ] Bondi rule observed if applicable
- [ ] Indigenous knowledge handled correctly (attributed or absent — never invented)
- [ ] Read aloud. Does it sound like someone who was there?

---

## A Note on What This Is For

These legends are not marketing copy. They are not SEO. They are not there to make the app feel premium.

They are there because every one of these pools has been here longer than the app, longer than the phones people carry to reach it, longer than the roads that lead to them. Some of them have been here longer than the concept of a road.

The Pool Legend is the smallest possible acknowledgement of that. It earns its place on the screen by being true and by being brief and by treating the reader as someone who can handle both.
